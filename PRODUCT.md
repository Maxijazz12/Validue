# VLDTA Product Vision

VLDTA is an assumption-testing engine for founders. It takes a business idea, extracts what the founder is betting on, pressure-tests those assumptions, and delivers a Decision Brief that says what survived and what did not.

## Core Thesis

Assumptions are the atomic unit of startup risk. The product's job is to make those assumptions visible, test them, and return verdicts with evidence and clear next steps.

## Product Truth

The brief is the product. Everything else is supporting machinery.

Founders should leave with:
- what is actually true
- what is most likely wrong
- what to test next

If the product produces vague feedback instead of real clarity, it is failing.

## Target User

Students and young founders, pre-build and pre-revenue.

Tone should feel direct, human, and brutally clarifying — not corporate, academic, or survey-like.

## Current Phase

Phase 1 — assumption extraction + Decision Brief.

Current blockers:
- end-to-end testing with real campaign data

## Build Order

### Phase 1
Make assumption extraction and the Decision Brief genuinely useful.

### Phase 2
Add analysis modules that improve the brief and increase decision quality with minimal extra complexity.

### Phase 3
Improve signal quality: better behavioral questions, better WTP probing, better assumption targeting.

### Phase 4
Add longitudinal validation across repeated rounds.

### Phase 5
Expand signal sources only after the core brief experience is clearly valuable.

## Do Not Build Yet

- expert respondent systems
- dynamic exhaustion logic
- per-lens payout logic
- dashboard-as-primary UX
- lens-based routing
- crowd-sourced competitive lens

## What VLDTA Must Not Become

- a survey builder
- a dashboard for reading raw responses
- a feedback marketplace with weak synthesis
- a product for every founder stage
- a product that mainly makes founders feel good

## Decision Rules

Before building, ask:

1. Does this improve the Decision Brief?
2. Does this sharpen assumption verdicts or next-step clarity?
3. Does this increase product usefulness without adding heavy system complexity?
4. Can this be shipped in a simple version first?
5. Is this proving usefulness or just optimizing plumbing?
6. Does this help founders reach uncomfortable truth faster?

## Success Criterion

The product is working if founders say:
"this would have changed my decision."

# Decision Brief: LocalBites

## The Idea

> "A food discovery app for 'hidden gem' restaurants — the places locals love but tourists and algorithms miss. Users submit and rate small, independent restaurants that aren't well-represented on Google Maps or Yelp. Think of it as a curated, community-driven food guide. Monetization: restaurants pay for a 'verified local favorite' badge and promoted placement. Target: foodies aged 22-35 in cities with 500k+ population."

---

## Assumptions Extracted

1. **Hidden gem restaurants are hard to find online** — Current platforms (Google Maps, Yelp, Instagram) fail at surfacing small, independent restaurants.
2. **People want community-curated recommendations over algorithmic ones** — Users trust "locals say this is great" more than star ratings or sponsored results.
3. **Restaurant owners will pay for a "verified local favorite" badge** — Small restaurant owners see value in a credibility badge and promoted placement.
4. **Foodies aged 22-35 will adopt a new food discovery app** — This demographic is underserved enough to switch from existing tools.
5. **User-generated submissions will produce quality content** — Community members will write useful reviews that are better than Yelp/Google reviews.

---

## Assumption Verdicts

### 1. Hidden gem restaurants are hard to find online
**Verdict: CONFIRMED (strongly)**

16 of 20 respondents described frustration with finding small restaurants online. The specific complaints were consistent: Google Maps prioritizes chains and high-review-count places, Yelp is pay-to-play, Instagram requires you to already follow the right accounts.

> *"Every time I search 'restaurants near me' I get the same 10 chains and the same 5 places with 2,000 reviews. I know there are better spots but I can't find them without asking someone."* — Respondent, 27, Chicago

> *"The best restaurants I've found were all through word of mouth. Never through an app."* — Respondent, 24, Austin

**Evidence strength:** 20 responses, strong consensus. Real pain point validated.

### 2. People want community-curated recommendations over algorithmic ones
**Verdict: CHALLENGED**

This is more complex than binary. 12 of 20 said they trust recommendations from "real people" more than algorithms. BUT when probed on behavior, only 4 said they actively seek out community recommendations. The other 8 described **passive discovery** — they want curated recommendations to appear without them having to search.

> *"I trust my friends' recommendations more than Yelp. But I'm not going to go to a website and browse through user reviews. I want someone to just tell me where to eat."* — Respondent, 31, NYC

> *"I follow a few local food Instagrammers. That's basically my restaurant discovery. I don't really 'search' for food."* — Respondent, 25, Portland

The distinction matters: users want **curated** (someone already filtered for quality) not **community** (browse raw user submissions). They want an editorial voice, not a forum.

**Evidence strength:** 20 responses. Trust in community is high but behavioral engagement with community content is low.

### 3. Restaurant owners will pay for a "verified local favorite" badge
**Verdict: REFUTED**

This was tested indirectly through respondents who work in or near the restaurant industry (4 of 20). All 4 were skeptical. Additionally, 14 of 20 respondents said a paid badge would **reduce** their trust in the recommendation.

> *"If I see 'verified local favorite' and it's a paid badge, that's just advertising pretending to be authentic. I'd trust it less."* — Respondent, 29, Denver

> *"My cousin owns a taco shop. He barely has time to update his Google listing. He's not paying for a badge on an app he's never heard of."* — Respondent, 26, LA

> *"The moment you let restaurants pay for placement, you become Yelp. That's exactly what you said you're not."* — Respondent, 33, Seattle

**Evidence strength:** 20 responses. The monetization model actively undermines the core value proposition. Users equate paid badges with inauthenticity.

### 4. Foodies aged 22-35 will adopt a new food discovery app
**Verdict: CHALLENGED**

7 of 20 said they'd try it. 13 said they wouldn't download a standalone app but would use it if it were: a newsletter, an Instagram account, a TikTok series, or a feature inside Google Maps. The barrier isn't interest — it's app fatigue.

> *"I have 3 food apps already and I use exactly zero of them regularly. I just use Google Maps and Instagram."* — Respondent, 28, SF

> *"If this was a weekly email with 3 restaurant picks in my city, I'd subscribe immediately."* — Respondent, 24, Chicago

**Evidence strength:** 20 responses. Interest is real but "app" is the wrong container for most users.

### 5. User-generated submissions will produce quality content
**Verdict: CHALLENGED**

Only 5 of 20 said they would actively submit restaurants. 9 said they'd rate places they've been but wouldn't write reviews. 6 said they'd consume but never contribute.

> *"I'm not writing reviews. I'll tap a heart button, max."* — Respondent, 23, Miami

The small contributor base means quality depends heavily on power users and editorial curation, not community volume. This is a 1/9/90 problem — 1% create, 9% engage, 90% lurk.

**Evidence strength:** 20 responses. Contribution asymmetry is well-documented and confirmed here.

---

## The Uncomfortable Truth

**The discovery problem is real. The business model is a trap.**

Users genuinely struggle to find great small restaurants. They genuinely trust local recommendations over algorithms. But the proposed monetization (paid badges for restaurants) directly contradicts the trust that makes the product valuable. The moment restaurants can pay for placement, the product becomes what it's trying to replace.

Additionally, "food discovery app" is not what this audience wants. They want curated recommendations delivered passively (newsletter, social media, or inside existing tools) — not another app to download and forget.

---

## Signal Summary

20 respondents, ages 22-33, across 8 US cities. Strong problem validation (hidden gems are hard to find). Strong trust signal (community > algorithm). But: users want passive curation not active browsing, won't download a standalone app, won't contribute content at scale, and paid restaurant badges actively undermine trust.

---

## Actionable Next Steps

1. **Kill the paid badge model immediately.** It contradicts your core value prop. Respondents explicitly said it would make them trust recommendations less.
2. **Explore alternative monetization:** Affiliate/reservation fees (partner with Resy/OpenTable), premium city guides ($4.99 one-time per city), or a Patreon/subscription model where users pay for the curation — not restaurants.
3. **Start as a newsletter or Instagram account, not an app.** 13 of 20 said they'd engage with curated food content in these formats. Build audience first, app second.
4. **Hire or become the editorial voice.** Users want someone to say "this is great, go here" — not a wiki where anyone can submit. Position as a curator, not a platform.
5. **Test in one city first.** Food discovery is hyper-local. A great guide for Chicago is more valuable than a mediocre guide for 20 cities.

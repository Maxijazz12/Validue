# Decision Brief: PitchPilot

## The Idea

> "An AI pitch deck builder for early-stage founders seeking investment. You input your business details — problem, solution, market size, traction, team — and it generates a complete investor-ready pitch deck. Not a template, an actual deck with tailored narrative, data visualizations, and competitive positioning. $29/month for unlimited decks, or $79 one-time for a single polished deck. Target: pre-seed and seed founders actively fundraising."

---

## Assumptions Extracted

1. **Building a pitch deck is a significant pain point for early founders** — Founders spend excessive time on decks and the process is frustrating.
2. **AI can generate investor-quality pitch narratives** — An AI-built deck can compete with decks made by humans (founders themselves or deck designers).
3. **Founders will pay $29/month or $79 one-time** — The pricing is within the willingness-to-pay range for this audience.
4. **The deck itself is the bottleneck to fundraising** — A better deck materially improves fundraising outcomes.
5. **Pre-seed/seed founders are the right first market** — This segment has the most pain and least resources.

---

## Assumption Verdicts

### 1. Building a pitch deck is a significant pain point
**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

17 of 20 respondents described the deck-building process as painful, time-consuming, or frustrating. Average reported time: 20-40 hours across multiple iterations. The specific complaints: blank page paralysis, uncertainty about what investors want, and constant reworking of narrative.

> *"I spent 3 weeks on my deck. Then an investor told me in 30 seconds it was wrong. I scrapped it and started over."* — Respondent, pre-seed founder

> *"The problem isn't making slides. The problem is figuring out the story. What order do I tell it? What do they care about?"* — Respondent, first-time founder

**Evidence strength:** 20 responses, near-unanimous. Pain is real and well-articulated.

### 2. AI can generate investor-quality pitch narratives
**Verdict: CHALLENGED (significantly)**

14 of 20 respondents had already tried using ChatGPT or similar tools to help with their deck. Of those 14, only 2 were satisfied with the output. The rest described the results as "generic," "obvious," or "missing the thing that makes my company interesting."

> *"ChatGPT gave me a deck that could be about any company. It had all the right sections but none of the insight. An investor would see through it in 10 seconds."* — Respondent, seed-stage founder

> *"I used it for the market size slide and the competitor landscape. Anything that required actual understanding of MY business, it was useless."* — Respondent, pre-seed founder

> *"The best decks I've seen from founders who raised money all had a unique narrative hook. AI can't find your hook — that's the founder's job."* — Respondent, angel investor (also founder)

The pattern: AI is useful for **specific sections** (market sizing, competitive landscape, financial projections template) but fails at **narrative construction** — the thing that actually matters to investors.

**Evidence strength:** 20 responses, 14 with direct experience using AI for decks. Strong pattern.

### 3. Founders will pay $29/month or $79 one-time
**Verdict: CONFIRMED (for the right product)**

11 of 20 said $79 one-time is reasonable. Only 4 said $29/month makes sense ("I'm only fundraising for 3-4 months, I don't need a subscription"). 5 said they'd only pay if they could see a demo output first.

> *"$79 for a deck that actually saves me 30 hours? That's nothing. But I need to see it's good before I pay."* — Respondent, pre-seed founder

> *"$29/month is weird. I need one deck. I'll pay once."* — Respondent, seed-stage founder

**Evidence strength:** 20 responses. One-time pricing validated. Subscription model does not match usage pattern.

### 4. The deck itself is the bottleneck to fundraising
**Verdict: REFUTED**

This is the critical finding. Only 3 of 20 said the deck was their biggest fundraising challenge. The top barriers cited:

- **Getting meetings / warm intros** (11 of 20)
- **Knowing which investors to target** (8 of 20)
- **Preparing for investor questions / objections** (6 of 20)
- **The deck itself** (3 of 20)

> *"No one has ever not invested because of my deck. They don't invest because they don't believe in the market or they don't think I can execute. The deck is just the thing that gets you in the door."* — Respondent, second-time founder

> *"I spent all this time on my deck and the investor didn't even look at it. He just asked me questions for 30 minutes."* — Respondent, pre-seed founder

> *"What I really need is someone to tell me which 20 investors to email and help me write the email that gets a response. The deck is step 5. Steps 1-4 are where I'm stuck."* — Respondent, first-time founder

**Evidence strength:** 20 responses. The deck is a necessary artifact but not the bottleneck. Access and preparation are.

### 5. Pre-seed/seed founders are the right first market
**Verdict: CONFIRMED (narrowly)**

Pre-seed founders (no traction, no revenue) are the most deck-anxious because they have the least to show. But they're also the least able to pay and the hardest to retain (they need one deck, then they're done). Seed-stage founders with some traction are better customers — they have budget and a more complex story to tell.

**Evidence strength:** 20 responses. Pre-seed is the most painful segment but seed-stage is the more viable customer.

---

## The Uncomfortable Truth

**You're solving the wrong problem. Founders don't need a better deck — they need to get in the room.**

The deck is necessary but not sufficient. 11 of 20 respondents said their #1 fundraising problem is getting warm intros and investor meetings, not building slides. A founder with a mediocre deck and a warm intro will outperform a founder with a perfect deck and no intro every time.

Meanwhile, AI-generated narrative — the core product promise — is the weakest part of AI deck tools. Founders who've tried it describe "generic" output that investors see through. AI works for structured sections (market sizing, competitive landscape) but fails at the narrative hook that makes a deck compelling.

---

## Signal Summary

20 respondents, mix of pre-seed (8), seed (7), and recently-funded founders (5). Deck-building pain is universal and real. Willingness to pay $79 one-time is validated. But: the deck is not the fundraising bottleneck (access is), AI narrative quality is low (specific sections are useful, whole-deck generation isn't), and subscription pricing doesn't match usage patterns.

---

## Actionable Next Steps

1. **Pivot from "AI deck builder" to "fundraising prep tool."** The bigger problem is getting meetings and being prepared for them. A tool that helps with investor targeting, warm intro pathways, and Q&A preparation is more valuable than a deck generator.
2. **If staying in deck space: sell section-level AI, not whole-deck generation.** Market sizing, competitive landscape, and financial projection slides are where AI adds real value. Narrative and story structure are the founder's job — help them do it better, don't replace them.
3. **Kill the subscription model.** One-time $79 (or tiered: $49 basic / $129 with feedback) matches the usage pattern. Founders need one deck, not "unlimited decks."
4. **Add a "see before you buy" demo.** 5 respondents explicitly said they need to see quality before paying. Let them input their idea and see a preview of one slide for free.
5. **Consider the real high-value product:** "We help you get your first 10 investor meetings." If you can deliver that, the deck becomes a feature, not the product. The willingness to pay for "guaranteed meetings with relevant investors" is dramatically higher than for "a pretty deck."

# Decision Brief: StudyBuddy

## The Idea

> "An AI-powered study group matching app for university students. Students input their courses, study style preferences, and schedule availability. The app matches them into small study groups (3-5 people) for each course. Includes a shared study space with AI-generated study guides, flashcards, and practice questions based on the course syllabus. Freemium model — free matching, $9.99/month for AI study tools. Target: undergraduate students at mid-to-large universities."

---

## Assumptions Extracted

1. **Students struggle to find study partners** — Finding compatible study groups is a real, unsolved pain point for undergrads.
2. **Schedule coordination is the main barrier** — The reason students don't form groups is scheduling conflicts, not lack of desire.
3. **AI study tools add enough value for $9.99/month** — Students will pay for AI-generated study materials on top of free group matching.
4. **Small groups (3-5) are the right format** — Students want small, consistent groups rather than large forums or 1-on-1 pairs.
5. **Students will adopt another app** — Students will download and regularly use a new app alongside their existing tools (Discord, GroupMe, Google Docs).

---

## Assumption Verdicts

### 1. Students struggle to find study partners
**Verdict: CONFIRMED (with nuance)**

15 of 18 respondents described difficulty finding study partners at some point. But the pain is concentrated: STEM and large lecture courses (200+ students) are where the problem is acute. Students in smaller programs (< 50 per cohort) said they already know everyone.

> *"In my organic chemistry class there are 300 people and I literally don't know a single one. I studied alone all semester and got a C."* — Respondent, Biology major

> *"I'm in a design program with 28 people. We all study together already."* — Respondent, Industrial Design

**Evidence strength:** 18 responses, high consistency across STEM respondents.

### 2. Schedule coordination is the main barrier
**Verdict: CHALLENGED**

Only 6 of 18 cited scheduling as the primary barrier. The top barrier cited by 11 respondents was **social anxiety / awkwardness of approaching strangers**, not logistics.

> *"I know I should study with other people but I don't know how to just ask someone. What if they think I'm weird?"* — Respondent, Computer Science

> *"It's not that I can't find a time. It's that I don't know anyone in the class well enough to ask."* — Respondent, Economics

Schedule coordination was the #2 barrier, relevant but not primary. The app's matching solves the social friction problem better than the scheduling problem — but the founder positioned it as a scheduling tool.

**Evidence strength:** 18 responses, clear pattern. Social friction > logistics.

### 3. AI study tools add enough value for $9.99/month
**Verdict: REFUTED**

14 of 18 respondents said they would not pay $9.99/month. 3 said "maybe" and 1 said yes. The dominant reason: **students already have free AI tools (ChatGPT, Quizlet AI, Notion AI)** and see no reason to pay for another one.

> *"I already use ChatGPT to make flashcards. Why would I pay for this?"* — Respondent, Psychology

> *"$10/month is a lot for a student. That's two meals. I'd need it to literally guarantee I pass."* — Respondent, Engineering

The 3 "maybe" respondents described a narrow use case: **exam-specific prep packs** tied to their actual professor's syllabus and past exams — not generic AI study tools.

**Evidence strength:** 18 responses, strong consensus. Pricing model does not work as described.

### 4. Small groups (3-5) are the right format
**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

12 of 18 preferred groups of 3-4. Reasons: accountability without social overwhelm, everyone can contribute, easy to schedule. Groups of 5+ were described as "too many freeloaders." Pairs were "too intense."

> *"4 is perfect. Everyone has to show up and actually do something."* — Respondent, Business

**Evidence strength:** 18 responses, clear preference.

### 5. Students will adopt another app
**Verdict: CHALLENGED**

10 of 18 said they would prefer this to be integrated into tools they already use (Discord bot, university LMS plugin) rather than a standalone app. 5 said they'd try a new app if the matching was good enough. 3 were strongly against installing anything new.

> *"If this was a Discord bot I'd use it today. If it's a new app, I'll download it and forget it exists."* — Respondent, Computer Science

**Evidence strength:** 18 responses, moderate pattern toward integration over standalone.

---

## The Uncomfortable Truth

**The matching is the product. The AI tools are not.**

Students will adopt a study group matching service because it solves a real social friction problem (not a scheduling problem). But they will not pay $9.99/month for AI study tools they can get free elsewhere. The monetization model is built on the wrong value driver.

The matching itself is high-value but hard to monetize directly — students expect social matching to be free (dating apps trained this expectation). The narrow monetization window is **exam-specific prep packs** tied to actual course content, not generic AI tools.

---

## Signal Summary

18 respondents, primarily STEM and business undergrads at universities with 10,000+ students. Strong problem validation in large-lecture environments. Social friction is the primary barrier, not scheduling. Group size preference (3-4) is clear. Monetization assumption is the critical failure point — free AI alternatives have collapsed willingness to pay for generic study tools.

---

## Actionable Next Steps

1. **Reposition as a social matching tool, not a study tool.** The value is "meet people in your class" — the studying is the context, not the product.
2. **Kill the $9.99/month AI tools plan.** Students won't pay. Explore: exam prep packs ($2.99 per course per exam), university licensing (per-seat to the institution), or freemium matching with premium group features (private rooms, persistent groups).
3. **Build as a Discord bot or LMS plugin first, not a standalone app.** Go where students already are. Standalone app = adoption cliff.
4. **Focus launch on STEM courses at large universities (200+ students per lecture).** That's where the pain is sharpest. Small programs don't need this.
5. **Test pricing on exam prep packs.** The 3 respondents willing to pay described wanting course-specific materials tied to their professor's syllabus. That's a different product than "AI study tools."
